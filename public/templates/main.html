2018 - 2020

I've been writing code for about as long as I can remember -- but the first time I began using software to solve problems with a real impact came around 2018, when I joined a program called the Pulsar Search Collaboratory, or the PSC. The PSC took processed data from the Green Bank Telescope (GBT) in West Virginia and let students try to find pulsars -- rotating neutron stars -- which are effectively the "corpses" of massive stars.

These stellar corpses spin remarkably fast and emit radiation from their poles -- if you take a look at the GIF on the right, you'll see roughly how they look in the radio part of the electromagnetic spectrum. As they rotate, their emission crosses our line of sight. We register this as a pulse or blip in our radio telescopes.

If we point a telescope at an unexplored region of the sky and collect data, we can try to filter for good candidates that might be new pulsars -- but that still leaves a lot of data for us! I decided to try and cut down the already-processed data we received using some different algorithmic approaches. You can take a look at the resulting paper and code here and here.
